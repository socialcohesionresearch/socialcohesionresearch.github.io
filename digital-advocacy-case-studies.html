Digital Advocacy Case Studies

Showcasing our Digital Advocacy Research

Overview:

Opinion accesses a database of almost 1 trillion posts and counting, dating back to 2010. Information is captured from Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, Tumblr, blog posts and forums. Every single public social media conversation globally has been captured and is available to us, and is reliable for analysis as far back as 2010.

We approach each project on a case-by-case basis and design an analytical strategy based on the objectives and the data we uncover. Research on Opinion is a fact finding mission – we can hold no pre-conceived notions about what is there, but what we can do is assure you that we have the best tools and the best possible analysts available to mine and make sense of relevant social media data.

To illustrate the tailorability and detail possible with Opinion, we offer a sample of case studies below, drawn from our publicly available reports:

Case Study One: Age identification: drawn from Brexit Re-Engage

We can accurately predict the age of a significant percentage of people engaging in a conversation. This enabled us to show that 18-35 year olds in the UK were remarkably disengaged from the Brexit conversation on social media, before and after the referendum.

From the 297 million posts generated during June of 2016 in the UK, we identified 7 496 582 related to Brexit. The Crimson Hexagon platform then used a complex algorithm to identify the age groups associated with those posts. We were able to identify the age of over 22% of those posts. This leaves us with 1 707 771 posts to analyse – a very healthy sample of public opinion on Brexit. The study focussed on the level of engagement of various age groups in the conversation, rather than the sentiment or opinion.

What we see above is a spectacular lack of engagement in the topic from the very people we would expect to be engaged in it – young, social media savvy voters, purportedly with a strong interest in staying in the EU. Looking at the age band 18-35 collectively – at their height (23-26 June) they contributed only 6.3% of the total conversation on Brexit. And within days of the referendum (27-30 June) this had dropped to a lamentable 3.5% of the conversation.

Between the 1 and the 22nd June - relative to their total online engagement - Britain’s youngest eligible voters (18-24 year olds) devoted only 0.27% of their online conversation to Brexit. This figure climbed to an unimposing high of 3.8% of their total conversation on the weekend after the referendum, and then plummeted to only 0.69% for the remainder of June 2016.

By contrast, the 35 plus group devoted 2.61% of their total online conversation to Brexit during the period 1-22 June (9 times as much as the youth), rose to 7.99% on the day of the referendum (twice as much as the youth) and then continued at 9.94% (14 times as much as the youth) for the remainder of June.

Citizens under the legal voting age were considerably more engaged than those aged 18-24 and 25-35, but far less engaged than those aged over 35.

Case Study 3: Affinities, drawn from Pound for Pound: Cryptocurrencies

In this piece we isolated the discussion around crypto currencies vs the pound in relation to Brexit.

Affinities analysis allows us to match the interests of the people contributing to the Pound conversation against the rest of twitter, do the same for the members of the Crypto Currency conversation and then compare the variances of each.

What we found was that those contributing to the Pound conversation tended to be more ‘establishment’ oriented – with much higher than average interest in stock markets, forex, David Cameron and finance.

Those contributing to the Crypto Currency conversation were demonstrably more forward thinking – and tended to be relatively more interested in Noam Chomsky, peer-to-peer sharing services(P2P), Bitcoin and Reddit that the global Twitter average.

Case Study 4: Boko Haram and the rise of fear

In this study we looked at various socio economic concerns in Nigeria with the aim of determining the impact the rise of Boko Haram had on how citizens perceived their personal threat levels.

We analysed over 1 BILLION posts over a 5 year study period, starting in January 1st 2010. Below is an overview categorisation of that data set:

Over the 5 year period, conversation pertaining to “Threat” comprised 11% of the conversation – the growth over that time period tells a more interesting story.

Grouping the data into two time periods - January 1st 2010 to the end of 2013 (before the rise of Boko Haram) and January 1st 2014 to October 31st 2015 (end of our study period).

What we found was that, prior to 2014, Threat constituted less than 1% of the conversation.

In comparison, post 2014 data shows that the percentage of conversation dedicated to Threat rose to a staggering 22% – eclipsing all other socio economic concerns over that period.

We have effectively shown that threat to personal safety as expressed on social media in Nigeria has grown over 22 fold since Boko haram rose to prominence.

Case Study 4: Boko Haram and sources of funding

Our final case study shows how we trained a monitor to isolate and then analyse social media posts related to funding for Boko Haram – resulting in a quantitative breakdown of who Nigerians perceive to be funding Boko Haram. There are many applications for this kind of research but strategically this information can allow policymakers to engage citizens in an impactful way.

We identified over 47 000 posts related to the topic over the 5 year period, and found that fully 54% of all Nigerian social media opinion points the finger at various levels of government or official power. This is split between Nigerian Government, State Leaders and Wealthy Nigerians(a term often referring to men of power).
